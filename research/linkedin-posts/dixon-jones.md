We've been measuring the wrong thing for AI search.

For thirty years, SEO has been about ranking pages. That mental model 
still holds in Google. But AI recommendation engines don't rank pages. 
They build associations. And those are not the same investment.

Here is a metric our industry needs: AI Topical Presence.

It measures how strongly, and how broadly, an AI model connects a 
brand to the commercial topics in its market. (Not whether you appear 
in AI answers, but WHAT you appear for).

Look at the chart.

This is a Waikay AI Competitive Map for the query "What are the best 
semantic SEO tools", and it tells a much more interesting story than 
Share of Voice alone.

Ahrefs has a "Topical Presence" score of around 60, but sits below 
5% AI Share of Voice. It is a Hidden Expert: the AI knows Ahrefs 
well, connects it to the right topics, but doesn't mention it as 
often as the leaders. That's a different problem to solve than a 
brand the AI barely knows at all.

inLinks sits just above the mid-tier threshold on Topical Presence, 
with reasonable Share of Voice. Room to grow, but solid foundations.

Now look at the bottom right corner. That quadrant is labelled 
"Hollow Presence". High Share of Voice, low Topical Presence. The 
AI mentions the brand often, but the associations are shallow. That 
brand is one model update away from a significant drop.

Topical Presence has three components:

Depth: how strongly the AI connects your brand to the topics that matter
Breadth: how many of those core topics your brand covers at all
Concentration: whether your visibility depends on just one or two 
topics, or is genuinely distributed

The combination tells you which lever to pull. A brand weak on 
Breadth needs to build new territory. A brand weak on Depth needs 
to strengthen what it already covers. A brand with high Concentration 
needs to diversify before the next model update does it for them.

And when a gap surfaces, the first question should not be "what should 
we write?" It should be: is this a content gap, a documentation gap, 
or a product gap? Each answer leads somewhere different.

Is AI Topical Presence a metric you'd find useful in your GEO or AI 
visibility work? I'm genuinely curious whether this framing maps to 
what you're seeing with clients.

Structural Gap Analysis:
A major new AIO study of 5,000 sites by Genie Jones and Fred 
Laurent, with help from Grant Simmons (SEO) and Nina on design, 
shows some really interesting findings about optimizing to get seen 
in AI responses and what happens when you have structural gaps. For 
example, they find an interesting AI double penalty. Structural gaps 
now suppress performance in two channels simultaneously: traditional 
search engines and AI-powered platforms such as ChatGPT, Perplexity, 
and Google SGE. Two documented case studies quantify this effect 
directly. There's a lot to take in, so you might want your favourite 
AI tool to condense it, but to wrap up, the report offers a six-point 
strategy to prioritize fixing gaps. These priorities are based on the 
specific business model, the competitive landscape, and how different 
types of gaps impact one another.

Why brands you never heard of appear in AI responses...

We ran an AI visibility audit for a well-known fintech firm last 
week. They rank page one on Google for their core keywords. Strong 
brand. Years of SEO investment.

Six of the ten brands appearing in ChatGPT recommendations for their 
market? They'd never heard of them.

This happens because AI doesn't learn your competitive landscape from 
analyst reports. It learns it from comment threads, comparison sites, 
blogs, and practitioner forums — sources most marketing teams have 
never audited.

A niche tool with a strong community presence beats a market leader 
with a locked-down content strategy when the searcher is specific 
about their needs.

Fred Laurent, our CTO, wrote a great breakdown of why — including a 
specific feature gap we found that nobody had noticed despite it 
being a mandatory compliance standard.
